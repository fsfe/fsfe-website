Software Patents

A future without software patents

FSFE is working towards a world where software does what users want it to do. Software patents create legal and financial risks that make it more difficult to develop and distribute software. They are a real threat to users' freedom. They harm innovation and hinder healthy competition in the software market.

What are software patents?

Patents are monopoly rights, granted by the state to the creator of a technical invention. When applied to software, patents block the idea behind a program and prohibits others from writing or distributing programs that work in a similar way, even if those programs are developed without knowledge of the patent. Unlike copyright, patents do not restrict the use of an expression or the code itself. Instead, patents restrict the use of certain ideas - in case of software these ideas consist of algorithms and mathematics.

Status quo in Europe

The European Patent Convention says that "programs for computers" cannot be patented. Despite this, the European Patent Office (EPO) has developed a doctrine saying that "computer implemented inventions" can be patented, and has issued possibly tens of thousands of patents on software. Due to the diversity of Europe's jurisdictions, enforcing these patents was so difficult that it was almost never done. This protected Free Software developers in Europe from the patent system's worst excesses.

Under extraordinary pressure from the Free Software community, the European Parliament in 2005 rejected the software patent directive, which would have allowed patents on computer programs outright. Since then, efforts to make software patentable have shifted towards creating a "unitary patent" system for Europe. Under this system, the EPO would have greater independence to implement its patent-maximalist doctrine. The unitary patent system is also meant to make it easier to enforce patents. This raises the threat level for Free Software developers considerably.

Forcing software to be incompatible

Software patents are used to prevent others from achieving compatibility or interoperability, notably in the area of standardisation. By blocking interoperability, a patent can be used to make competition impossible and to prevent developers from writing code to offer equivalent software under better licence terms, such as Free Software licences.

Software patents hinder innovation

The harm to software innovation has been shown in various studies, for example by Nobel Prize winning economist Eric S. Maskin. The reason is that software development is incremental: To write a useful software package, you build your work of innovative features on top of old features. If the use of old features is restricted by patents, you are blocked in innovation.

Patents are unlike Copyright

Patents are completely unlike copyright. To avoid someone's copyright, you just have to ignore their software. But you cannot ignore patents. Patent restrictions apply to your work, even if you are not aware of the patent.

Patent wars encourage monopolies

The biggest software companies hold the biggest software patent-portfolios to keep competitors out of their market. A large consortium of partners opposing the European Software Patent Directive [0] was possible, because software patents hinder all kinds of software development: free and proprietary, commercial and non-commercial.

Current threats: the unitary patent proposals

The European Commission is working on a unitary patent system for the European Union. This system includes enforcement of the European Patent Office (EPO) with an unchecked power to decide upon the grant and validity of patents. Plans also include the creation of a central European patent court with patent lawyers as judges. Given an unchecked power of the EPO with a new pro-patent court, software patents would most likely become enforceable.
