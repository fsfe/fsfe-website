Revisiting Sony Rootkit 10 years later

Introduction

Imagine you buy a music CD in a store. You go home and put it into your computer to listen to it. Without your knowledge a program is installed. This program secretly checks whether you start programs to copy CDs, and if so forces them to stop. It also slows down your computer and open security holes which can be used by others to attack you. That is what happened 10 years ago if you bought one of 25 million music CDs from Sony. This attack by Sony on people's computers was discovered on 31 October 2005 and was later referred as the "Sony rootkit" program. It affected more than 550,000 networks in more than one hundred countries, including thousands of US military and defense networks.

Sony's rootkit provides a very clear example what harm can occur when companies try to control users' behaviour. Not only do these restrictions invade user's privacy, limit their property rights, and open up their computers to malicious attacks; these restrictions do not even achieve their intended purpose of stopping piracy.

Even though the Sony rootkit is now 10 years old, hurtful digital restrictions are everywhere. In recent news, the VW scandal provides another example of how restrictions allow manufacturers manipulate software for their own benefit.

Facts and Contexts

  • The strongest characteristic of modern computing is that they are universal machines; you can program them to do numerous tasks according to the user's wishes. The diversity of their functions allows users to discover new purposes and improve existing ones. However, companies who produce digital products feel threatened by this. They want to limit what we as a society can do with those machines in order to better fit their old business model.
  • One way for content providers to control how users use their computers is to implement technical restrictions on software, otherwise called Digital Restrictions Management (DRM) or "Technological Protection Measures" (TPM). These restrictions take away users' control over their own devices and their rights to manipulate legally purchased content any way they want to, in the name of protecting against unauthorised copying, or users' own security.
  • Restrictions remain an omnipresent characteristic of the modern age, and October 31st commemorates a historic day in the rise (and temporary fall) of digital restrictions. It marks the 10 year anniversary of the Sony BMG rootkit fiasco.
  • On this day back in 2005, tech security expert Mark Russinovich published his discovery on his blog 1 about a piece of spyware, known as a rootkit, that secretly installed itself on his computer when he added a recent Sony CD album to his computer.
  • When a Sony BMG CD was played on a computer, users would have to download Sony's proprietary music player, in order to listen to the CD. Then Sony installed a hidden rootkit program and used it to spy on users, their listening habits, and share that information with Sony, as well as prevent other third party audio programs from reading the disc.2
  • The rootkit was secretively installed on user's computer operating systems, and in the process created additional security flaws which opened the doors for other, more malicious attacks. Even if user detected the rootkit, safely uninstalling it without damaging your computer was another problem.3
  • Sony BMG's president, Thomas Hesse, dismissed the issue completely, and was quoted saying " Most people, I think, don't even know what a Rootkit is, so why should they care about it?"4
  • Sony later had to reveal that the rootkit was loaded onto roughly 25 million CDs and infected more than 550,000 networks in more than one hundred countries, including thousands of US military and defense networks.5
  • Naturally the public was outraged to learn what companies were secretly doing to their personal property and Sony was forced to settle numerous lawsuits and repair customers' trust as soon as possible. 6
  • Despite the fallout of Sony's DRM experiment, 10 years later, restrictions on users' personal property are more prevalent than ever. But not just music or video downloads are restricted, restrictions are commonly found in legitimately purchased ebooks, video game hardware, and all manner of proprietary software. It has even found ways into our cars,7 and coffee machines.8 DRM is everywhere because software is everywhere.
  • Technological restrictions on the legitimate use of devices are dangerous because they are slowly transforming our computers from being general purpose machines with diverse capabilities, to being a singular device with limited scope of power. Private companies limit computers' functionality because it is better for business when users are locked in to a particular service provider.
  • When users are locked in by restrictions from content providers and oppressive copyright legislation, society suffers because people lose out on the possibilites of innovating and experimenting with new products or services, as well as their ability to fix and improve their own devices. By trying to restrict the use of device or content for one specific case (i.e. unauthorised copying or to prevent outsiders from accessing the device), companies prevent to use computer for all other legitimate purposes that users may be entitled to. This is a major obstacle for future innovations and destroys the computer as a general purpose machine. Furthermore, these restrictions do not differentiate between legitimate or illegal manipulations performed on the computer by its users, imposing blanket constraints on everyone. As a consequence, no one beside the manufacturer has control over machines that control our lives.

FSFE Demands

"We can't trust companies to always do what is best for society; consumers, companies, organisations and governments need to be in control of their own devices. We need to voice our opinions and support legislation that empowers users with the right to tinker with their own property" said FSFE's President Matthias Kirschner.

He continues "As we commented on the EU Parliament's copyright reform (Reda report), some progress is being made towards harmonising restrictions exceptions in EU legislation that would make it harder for companies to use digital restrictions on whatever technology they want. But there are definitely improvements that can be made. Now we ask the Commission to go even further along this path and make it clear that no exception to copyright should ever be limited by DRM."

  • FSFE is calling on lawmakers to safeguard the right to tinker for everyone The right to tinker makes sure that the owner of every device is allowed to replace or supplement the software in that device if they so choose, thereby empowering owners to control their own property. To ensure this protection, FSFE asks the Commission to propose legislation strengthening computer owner's rights, by requiring that computer owners must be enabled to modify and exchange the software and hardware on any computing device they buy or otherwise have permanent possession of, and afterwards have the right to sell it again.
  • Many manufacturers today add technological restrictions that prevent device owners from changing their devices, or having someone do so for them. This can be in breach of the licences on the devices (as with Free Software/Open Source Software licences, which grant the rights to use, study, share, and improve the software for any purpose). The right to tinker must also be coupled with a legal provision that prevents technological restrictions of the same right. For this reason the FSFE asks the Commission to propose legislation to ensure that consumers can make use of digital goods which they have acquired within the full scope of copyright exceptions and limitations
