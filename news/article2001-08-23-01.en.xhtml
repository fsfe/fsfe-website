Free Software Experts

Who wasn't, at least once, depressed because so called Free Software projects were financed despite the fact that they had nothing to do with Free Software ? Europe will soon finance new projects and we can prevent these accidents by becoming External Experts. It will then be more difficult to claim that nothing could be done : our skills are required to validate the candidate projects.

The Fifth Framework Programme (FP5) of the European Commission needs experts. Among the candidates who proposed themselves thru the online application form, only a few of them have a real understanding of what Free Software is and how it works. Candidates will be eligible, but only a few will really be solicited. In every case the experts only speak for themselves, not for their employer nor for organizations they belong to. Early October 2001 a number of projects will be sent for evaluation in the Creating a user-friendly information society framework.

Who will then be able, regardless of technical matters, to judge if a project that calls itself Free Software is not a marketing attempt to use a fashionable word ? The candidates know that the European Commission wishes to encourage Free Software projects because of their intrinsic qualities of freedom, independence and sharing. The temptation will be great for them to pretend they have the technical and human resources necessary to create and maintain a Free Software project event if they don't have the slightest idea of what it really means. If no expert have a real knowledge of Free Software project development, how will the European Commission be able to sort that out ?

An example demonstrates this. Let's pretend that the GNU PKI project and the GNU GLUE project submit proposals that are equally good technically speaking (regardless of the fact that they deal with different subjects). Let's pretend again that the commission has to make a choice between the two, without the help of experts knowing Free Software. Their favor will probably go to GNU PKI since they do a better job at marketing themselves.

It turns out that people knowing Free Software would detect a few strange things on GNU PKI. First of all it is not a package of the GNU project, despite their name. When one knows that the GNU project is the foundation of every Free Software operating system currently distributed (Debian, RedHat, Mandrake etc.), this mistake implies a disturbing ignorance. Free Software is a matter of communicating with heterogeneous development groups, this is therefore a point that plays against them. In addition, the security expert of GNU PKI, Mr Eduard Tric, never participated in a Free Software development and no package of the software developed so far is available.

GNU PKI is therefore obviously launched by people who are new to Free Software. One cannot expect all the cases to be so simple. Evaluating a project, for the European Commission or for your own company often requires a more subtle study. When dealing with Free Software it is mandatory to carefully evaluate the legal protection because various licenses are used and many companies are involved. Their ability to cooperate with development teams on the network, their ability to establish a dialog and their current involvement in the Free Software community is also of great consequences. Those points are not technical, they don't have an equivalent in non-free software projects and can only be evaluated by people actively involved in Free Software.

Loïc Dachary member of the GNU project
