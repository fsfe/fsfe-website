A restriction on technological restrictions

The right to install other software on your computing devices

Safecast is a global project to map radiation data from around the world and release this information openly. When the project just started out, they used radiation meters, together with their own custom software and a few clever tweaks, to allow anyone to participate in the data collection. This kind of ingenuity is something we see every day: the ability to re-purpose or adapt existing technology by replacing or supplementing its software. While Safecast is a special case in this, we all depend on the possibility to install or replace programs that we use every day, to increase the security, privacy or performance of our devices, or just because we happen to like a different program on our laptop, tablet, phone, router, television, car, or other device.

Today at the International Day Against DRM, the undersigned organisations are calling on lawmakers to implement legislation that safeguards the right to tinker for everyone. To make sure that everybody is allowed to replace or supplement the software in any device, if the owner of that device wants it, thereby empowering device owners to control their own property. Many manufacturers today add technological restrictions that prevent device owners from changing their devices, or having someone change it for them. Even when those rights are granted by virtue of the software licenses being used by the software on the devices (as with Free Software/Open Source Software). It's clear that any right to tinker must also be coupled with a legal provision that prevents technological restrictions on the same right.

To successfully guarantee that device owners are in control of their own technology, the following organisations ask for a right to tinker for everyone, and a restriction on technological restrictions.
