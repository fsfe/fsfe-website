Free Software Foundation Europe

Frequently Answered Questions (FAQ)

  • Q: How can I help you?

    Being a non-profit, in many areas volunteer-driven Non-Governmental Organisation (NGO), our resources — and hence our capabilities to do our work — largely depend on volunteer contribution and donations.

    So if you would like us to do more or simply enable us to keep working, please help us either with your volunteer work, a donation or both.

  • Q: Why do you not have an "associate membership" like the FSF North America?

    A: FSF Europe has considered this in the past - in fact we have been receiving requests for something like this as since 2001. We did consider it, but have decided against it for the FSF Europe.

    Our main reason for deciding against associate membership was that we felt it was problematic to call normal donors "members" if they do not get the right to vote. This is not so unusual in the United States, especially for U.S. book clubs and such, but for a European organisation working in the political field it did not seem fitting.

    We are considering other possible alternatives to give people some goodies for supporting the FSF Europe, though.

    The sponsoring buttons were a first step, but there will be more.

  • Q: Who are the members that make up FSF Europe?

    A: People from different European countries, usually with a long history in Free Software. A list of members is available here.

  • Q: How can I become member of the FSF Europe?

    A: Warning: It is possible that the question you really meant to ask is "How can I get involved in the FSF Europe?" -- please read on to decide for yourself.

    As the major Free Software Foundations (such as the FSF Europe) maintain the legal aspects of the GNU Project including maintaining the GPL, LGPL, GFDL licenses or legal documents such as the Fiduciary Licence Agreement (FLA), membership in the FSF cannot be entirely open. Otherwise we'd jeopardise our ability to defend these against hostile takeover.

    Also because of the often highly political and legal nature of our work, we sometimes need to find a compromise that we may not be entirely happy with, but that our members are then bound to.

    Consequently, membership in the FSF Europe requires a high level of personal dedication and willingness to rate the needs of Free Software above personal interests. Not everyone is willing to or capable of making that commitment.

    When people are willing to make such a commitment, new members are appointed by act of the general assembly when 3/4 of all existing members are convinced the applicant understand the long term issues of Free Software and wants to devote a large part of his or her life to this task. We hope to have 3-4 members (but not much more) from each European country at some point.

    Should you consider this for yourself, we recommend starting to work with us so we can get to know each other.

    So regardless of whether you really meant to ask this question or whether you just wanted to know how to get involved, it might be useful to read on at "How can I get involved in the FSF Europe?"

  • Q: How can I get involved in the FSF Europe?

    A: There are several ways to get involved in the activities of the FSF Europe. If you just wish to get active doing some things, you might have a look at the Help Section.

    If you wish to work with us, you could either seek direct contact or get involved in the FSF Europe through working in the Associate Organisations.

    There is no limit but your engagement on how involved you can become in the internal workings of the FSF Europe once we get to know each other.

  • Q: Someone claimed to be working for you, is this true?

    A: We have many people who work with and for us on a regular basis, so even if you get contacted by someone with an email address that does not seem related, this person may be working in our name. Chances are you will find that person on our list of Permanent Volunteers. Otherwise please ask us if you want to be sure.
