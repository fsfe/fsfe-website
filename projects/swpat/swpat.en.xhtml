Software Patents in Europe

Expected events:

The European Commission is expected to release some information related to the public consultation around June 2001.

The European Commission is expected to issue a proposal for a new european directive related to software patents in May 2001.

Status report:

In Europe, the CONVENTION ON THE GRANT OF EUROPEAN PATENTS (EUROPEAN PATENT CONVENTION) (published in 1973 and revised December, 17th 1991) explicitly excludes programs for computers from the field of patentable inventions (article 52.2). This convention is voted by representatives of each country and is binding for all of them. Some countries are not part of the european union although most of them are. The European Patent Office is a private company who is in charge of implementing the European Patent Convention, it is not a governemental agency.

Despite of the exception related to computer programs, the European Patent Office granted a large number of patents that are de-facto software patents. Some of these patents are listed in the European Software Patents: Assorted Examples. Each of them can be checked at their original location.

In June 1997, the European Commission decided to reconsider the patentability of programs in a Green Paper named Promoting innovation through patents. In November 2000 the European Patent Office proposed a new version of the European Patent Convention in which the exception related to computer programs was removed. This modification was postponed by the representatives of the european countries because the process of evaluating if software patents are desirable is not over ( European Patent Office press release , Act revising the convention on the grant of european patents in which the exception was maintained).

When the time comes, government officials will take their decision based on the following factors:

  • Official directions from their government that are bound by the directives of the European Commission.
  • The outcome of the public consultation that was open from October 2000 to December 2000.
  • The study conducted by a private company that concludes in favor of software patents (???? 1999). This study was ordered by the European Commission.
  • Interpretation of the TRIPS treaty.
  • Their personal belief.

In order for software patents to become legal in Europe, it is enough to remove the exception related to computer programs from the European Patent Convention.

To prevent software patents to become legal in Europe, the exception related to computer programs must be preserved and the Art 52 of the European Patent Convention reworded. In addition the European Patent Office must revoke all de-facto software patents that were granted.

This status report gives you a broad view of the current situation. The problem of software patents is complex because it requires a strong legal background to be fully understood, involves many countries, influences the life of every european citizen and the economical activities of every company using or developing software. Because of this, people and organizations have to elaborate a large set of strategies to prevent software patents to become legal.

The most proeminent actor against software patents is the EuroLinux Alliance who deserves all the credit for the decision made by the officials during the Diplomatic Conference held in November 2000.
