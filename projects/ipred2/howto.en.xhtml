Aims of this document

This document should give you the information you need to start working on getting IPRED2 fixed. The main questions this document tries to answer are: "Who do I write to?" and "What can I ask them to do?" This document ends with informal suggestions for how this proposal could be amended. Note that these are not our proposals, just some ideas which we will look into.

If this document doesn't have what you're looking for, try asking on the IPRED2 mailing list (a publicly archived list, hosted by FSFE).

Who can directly influence the proposal?

The IPRED2 proposal (com(2005)276) has two parts: pages 2 to 8 of the proposal are a Directive, and pages 9 to 15 are a Council Framework Decision.

To check the current status of the proposal, see the Directive's entry in the Legislative Observatory, or its entry in the PreLex, and see The Framework Decision's entry in the Legislative Observatory, or its entry in the PreLex.

For the Directive, the EP and the Council have approximately equal power and they accept/reject amendments to the Directive based on majority votes. The focus usually switches back and forth between these two institutions. The European Parliament will probably vote first, then the Council, then the Parliament again. The timeline is not predictable, and at the time of writing this document, the date for even the first vote (which will be by the Parliament) has not been set. If you need to ask someone about which body is currently most involved in the Directive, you could ask on the above mentioned mailing list.

For the Council Framework Decision, unanimity in the Council is required. The European Parliament has an advisory role, which is of limited influence.

The European Parliament

The most influential MEPs for both parts of this Proposal, are the MEPs that are members of the Legal Affairs committee - usually called "JURI". This committee has been assigned responsibility for both parts of this proposal because it is the expert group on legal matters.

Before the European Parliament votes on this Directive, JURI will have a vote on the proposed amendments, and the result will be passed to the rest of the MEPs as a non-binding recommendation. See: the list of the members of JURI. The person in JURI who is responsible for this Directive (the "Rapporteur") is Nicola Zingaretti (PSE, Italy).

As well as being able to vote for a recommendation before the Parliament's vote, JURI members are influential because other MEPs expect them to have a good understanding of legal matters, and they're expected to have paid particular attention to this Directive since their committee is responsible for it. For these reasons, many non-JURI MEPs will ask JURI MEPs for advice on this issue. So it's important that JURI members understand the problems with this Directive and Framework Decision.

In addition three other committees will draft Opinions. The three committees are Industry, Research and Energy (called "ITRE"), Internal Market and Consumer Protection (called "IMCO"), and Civil Liberties, Justice and Home Affairs (called "LIBE"). While JURI is the committee that is expert on this issue, these three other committees are experts in topics which are affected by or related to the topic of the Directive. So, these don't carry as much weight as JURI's Recommendation, but they still have an extra voice that we can ask them to use. See: the list of members of ITRE, and the list of members of IMCO, and the list of members of LIBE.

The Council

The Council is made of one Minister nominated by the government of each member state. Council meetings are chaired by the Minister of the country which has the presidency. Governments choose their representative based on the topic of the legislation. Your country's representative cannot be predicted with certainty, but it is highly likely that the Minister chosen by your country's government will be the same Minister that represented your country for the software patents issue.

The likelihood is reinforced by the Council Documents register page for this proposal which describes the subject matter as being "DROIPEN, PI" (which refers to "Substantive Criminal Law", "Intellectual Property") - topics that are likely to to be decided on by the Competitiveness Council - the same group of Ministers that debated the software patents directive.

For example, the Republic of Ireland's representative was the Minister of Enterprise, Trade, and Employment. During the software patents issue, this was Mary Harney. She has since been replaced by Micheál Martin. So Irish people should try to make Minister Martin aware of the problems with the proposal.

The Minister will usually consult a National body. For example, in Ireland, the Minister for Enterprise, Trade, and Employment, and he will consult the Department of Enterprise, Trade, and Employment. In the UK it is the Patents and Trademarks Office. Some countries may have further governmental bodies for reviewing EU legislative proposals. In the Republic of Ireland, there is a government sub-committee called the EU Scrutiny Committee. (If you can provide information of relevent bodies in other countries, please email the above-mentioned IPRED2 mailing list).

Getting into dialogue with the Council has proven in the past to be more difficult than getting into dialogue with the MEPs. So it is more important to try this, since few will succeed.

Explaining our concerns

A lesson from the software patents directive is that was have to be precise about how we explain ourselves. Words we use can have completely different meaning when discussing EU legislation For example, when we say "technical", we mean "complex" - when EU legislation says "technical" it means "is patentable".

We also have to be concise. MEPs love 1-page letters and bullet points. 2 pages is the maximum they can be expected to read. If you're writing a letter and you feel that a topic has to be explained in detail, you could include a summary in the letter and mention that for a fuller explanation, the MEP can contact you - or can contact someone such as FSFE.

FSFE's Brussels Representative, Ciaran O'Riordan could be available for in-person meetings with MEPs or their assistants.

To friendly MEPs, particularly those who are not on the above mentioned committees, a very short letter is recommended. Probably starting with "This is just a short email to let you know about about a new directive: 2005/0127(COD) (part of com(2005)276)...".

Always mention the number of the document you're writing about. The Directive is 2005/0127(COD), and the Council Framework Decision is 2005/0128(CNS). The complete proposal - the document containing the Directive and the Framework Decision - is com(2005)276.

Make sure you sound calm and thoughful. In the software patents direcitive, some MEPs complained about angry or aggressive phone calls and letters.

Some usable bullet points

Here are some bullet points which may be useful for explaining our concerns in an email:

  • The justifications are not mirrored by the text. The justifications are mostly about organised crime and the prevention of health and safety risks - but the actual text of the Articles includes no such limit. (The Framework Decision does, but the Directive doesn't.)
  • Don't rely on examples from the USA. Many EU member states already have criminal sanctions for copyright infringement. So we need to use European examples.
  • "Commercial scale" has no definition.
  • The Directive talks about "intentional infringements", but there is no explanation as to whether this means that the infringer had to have been planning the infringement, or just planning the act which turned out to be an infringement. For example, if I write some software and I accidentally make unauthorised use of a trademark. Is that an intentional infringement? My act of writing software was intentional, but it wasn't my intention to infringe the trademark.
  • The Directive, as published by the Commission, will fail it's objective of harmonising EU law. Being extremely vague, it will
    1. give insufficient guidelines for legislators transcribing it into national law
    2. require further clarification by the national courts
    3. probably cause yet another Directive later on to harmonise the mess created.
  • Unlike previous decades, in the information age, the general public participates in the production and distribution of copyrighted materials. Trademarks can be used very easily, but some uses are legal and others are illegal. The public will make mistakes. Civil penalties are sufficient for this. Patents and copyright are also easily infringed by the public today.
  • Criminal law is commonly reserved for acts which carry no doubt among the general public about their criminality. Murder, rape, kidnapping, and assault is not a grouping which should have copyright infringement added to it.
  • The threat of criminal sanctions will reduce the public's freedom, not by expanding legislation but by turning grey areas and areas of uncertainty into no-go areas - and business no-go areas.
  • The almost 50,000 software patents that the European Patent Office has granted, still exist, but European programmers can be confident that >95% of litigation based on such patents will fail. However, if that 5% threat carried a criminal record and the possibility of jail time, large fines, closure of business, and other penalties. Software development could be chilled.
  • Since the European Copyright Directive, as well as regulating copying, copyright now regulates how the public can use technology to access copyrighted material. This has created many grey areas about how software developers can examine their competitors products to make interoperable systems and about what software is legal (and what software is "a protection circumvention device"). Also, by making useful acts illegal, it has created new illegal-but-tolerated acts (such writing software to play standard DVDs).
  • Copyright law also restricts the publication of technical information. The misunderstanding should be clarified, "copyright" as it exists today, is not only about copying information, but also about publication of and reading information.
  • Most of the harm of this Directive is caused by it's lack of precision. Such unclarity should not be pushed on the public. Those behind this Directive should not be afraid of being precise about what they are interested in seeing criminalised.
  • In the software world where there are many areas of legal uncertainty, and a lot of partisan misinformation about what is legal. The combination of harsh punishments and misinformation will scare the public away from many activities that are legal.

What sort of amendments do we want

FSFE have not compiled a formal package of amendments, but here are some ideas which should be considered. I must emphasise that these are informal suggestions, to aid understanding of what we hope to achieve. Our actual proposed amendments will surely be different to these.

Suggestions of improvements or alternatives are sought. Please email the above-mentioned IPRED2 mailing list.

To the Directive

Article 3

"On a commercial scale"
This wording should be kept because it is the wording of the TRIPS agreement. However, it must be given an definition. The definition should require that the action be "for-profit", and that it's commercial harm be "direct". Otherwise almost every act could fall under a loose definitin such as: indirect actions which cause loss of potential financial earnings.
"Intentional infringement"
This is a difficult term to define. The problem of lack of clarity can probably be solved by defining this as "in conscious violation of the law". Also, this definition will solve the problem that an infringement could otherwise be called intentional simply because it is part of an intentional activity (such as writing software or reading a webpage). However, if being conscious of the law changes your act from a civil offense to a criminal offense - reading about law becomes dangerous. Also, rights-holders could have the power to convert infringers from civil offenders to criminals simply by sending a threat letter (even though the recipient doesn't know the validity of it). TRIPS uses the term "willful".
"aiding and abetting"
These words may have to be completely removed. If not, they will have to be qualified even more so than actually making an infringement. For example, they would have to also be doing willfully, and directly for profit.

Article 4

Article 4 of the Directive lists what punishments must be available for those who commit one of the crimes created by Article 3. Article 4 is therefore not as important for us. If Article 3 is limited so that it doesn't add potential criminality to software development, Article 4 will be of no consequence to software developers.

To the Framework Decision

Article 4

"holders of intellectual property rights are ... allowed to assist the investigations carried out by joint investigation teams"
For this, "assist" should be defined as a requirement for rights-holders to provided information which they claim to have regarding an infringement.

Article 6

"ensure the possibility of initiating investigations into, or procescution of ... are not dependent on a report or accusation"
This enables national enforcement agencies to enforce people's "intellectual property rights" - even when the rights holder does not request it. It could probably be fixed by limiting it (using a wording borrowed from Article 3 of the Framework Decision) to infringemtns which are believed to have been "committed under the aegis of a criminal organisation".
