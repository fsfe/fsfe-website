Software Patents in Europe

The European Patent Convention (published 1973) explicitly states that the field of programs for computers, i.e. software, is excluded from patentability.

There is reason behind this: while a program in its concrete form is protected from plagiarism by copyright law, a patent would protect the mathematical and logical methods used by the program. Therefore, a patent would manifest a monopoly of specific calcuation methods, would make mathematical laws, logical rules and business processes the suable propriety of companies.

The United States of America have already traced out the path: Since software patents are legal there, it came to a flood of patent applications by big companies. They can easily come up with a suit for patent infrigment against any small or medium enterprise trying to get into the software market, and the unpleasant competitor is ruined due to the high process costs, or it is simply bought.

Currentyl, Europe highly profits from the burden the U.S. has put upon themselves: the small and medium software companies have made Europe a central player in innovation, while in the U.S., innovative software development is limited to a few monopolists.

However, the European Union faces a growing pressure from the outside to allow software patents like the U.S. does. On the one hand, the European Patent Office (which is not controlled by any government or the EU) has granted about 30,000 software patents despite the fact they are disallowed by the European Patent Convention, on the other hand, a directive on the "patentability of computer implemented inventions" is being prepared with the intent to legalize those (granted, but currently not legally valid) patents.

Current Status

The European Commission's original proposal for the directive, which has been heavily criticised, would have allowed unlimited patenting of software, calcuation rules and business processes.

September 24, 2003 the European Parliament (which is elected directly by the people in the EU) voted on the commission's proposal. The Members of the Parliament decided to pass a heavily modified version of the directive which would prevent patentability of pure abstract software without a technical context. This decision to value the needs of European economy higher than the wishes of the excessively lobbying global companies was widely regarded as a victory for democracy.

Die Entscheidungsprozesse in der Europäischen Union sind ausgesprochen kompliziert und aufwändig. Nach der Entscheidung des Parlamentes ist nun der Ministerrat an der Reihe, dem Parlament zuzustimmen oder einen Gegenvorschlag zu präsentieren. Allerdings scheinen in diesem Gremium die Befürworter grenzenloser Patentierbarkeit eine sehr starke Position zu haben. Nach einer monatelangen Verschleppung der Sache wurde nun hinter verschlossenen Türen ein sogenannter "Kompromiss" ausgearbeitet, der in Wirklichkeit inhaltlich mit der ursprünglichen, vom Parlament abgelehnten Fassung völlig übereinstimmt.
The decision process in the European Union is very complex. After the parliament's decision, it's the turn of the council to agree with the parliament or to present a counterproposal. Unfortunately, the supporters of unlimited patentability seem to have a strong position in the council. After having delayed the issue for months, the council has now prepared a so-called "compromise" which is essentially identical with the original proposal that was rejected by the parliament.

The Permanent Representatives Committee (COREPER), which prepares the decisions of the council and is bound to directives from the national governments, has already accepted this version. The directive proposal will be decided on by the council on May 17 without further discussion. It looks very much like the council will accept this proposal to allow unlimited patentability of software.

The Most Important Items Of The Directive

  • General patentability of software: While the version that the European Parliament agreed upon negates the general patentability of software and only allowes software patent in a very limited way for clearly defined exceptions, the council's version declares a general patentability of software and tries to calm down critics by fictitious restrictions that can be circumvented in praxis without problems.
  • Interoperability: The European Parliament states in its version of the directive that data exchange or data conversion ("interoperability") can never constitute a patent infrigment. The council wants to strike out this rule and therefore allow patenting and monopolizing file formats.
  • Definition of "technical": The version of the parliament defines clearly that only technical inventions are patentable, and defines the word "technical" as "applying of nature forces". Opposed to that, the council did not only strike out the definition of "technical", it also decided that inventions should also be patentable if they show no technical progress at all!
